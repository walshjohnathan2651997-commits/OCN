## 1. RIGOURATE paragraph

RIGOURATE [James et al., 2026] introduces a two-stage multimodal framework for quantifying scientific overstatement: a fine-tuned reranker retrieves supporting evidence passages from the body of a scientific paper, and a fine-tuned model assigns a continuous overstatement score in [0, 1] to each (claim, evidence) pair, accompanied by a brief justification. The framework is trained on over 10K claim-evidence sets extracted from ICLR and NeurIPS papers, with overstatement scores generated by a panel of eight LLM annotators, calibrated using peer-review comments, and validated through targeted human evaluation. RIGOURATE operationalises *evidential proportionality* — the degree to which a claim's wording is grounded in the paper's own methods and results — and reports consistent improvements over zero-shot baselines on both evidence retrieval and overstatement scoring. We position our work as complementary: where RIGOURATE produces a broad-coverage scalar severity signal, we decompose the same underlying phenomenon into a four-class relation taxonomy that isolates the high-risk action-overclaim boundary class.

## 2. ForceBench paragraph

ForceBench [X et al., 2026] provides a contrastive evidence-force calibration stress test that fixes the cited passage and compares a *calibrated claim* against a *force-raised claim* along five force dimensions: relation, modality, scope, temporal validity, and numeric specificity. The benchmark supplies a monotonic preference signal that rewards models for ranking the calibrated claim above the force-raised claim, enabling fine-grained assessment of whether models are sensitive to specific kinds of force raising. ForceBench's contrastive paired-claim design is complementary to our single-instance relation-typing formulation: ForceBench tests whether a model can *prefer* a calibrated claim over a force-raised claim, while we test whether a model can *type* a single claim-evidence pair into one of four evidence-sufficiency relations. The two formulations cover different failure modes — preference versus typing — and a complete evidence-calibration system would benefit from both.

## 3. CLAIM-BENCH paragraph

CLAIM-BENCH [Y et al., 2025] evaluates scientific claim-evidence extraction and validation across full papers, requiring models to identify which passages in a paper support or refute a given claim and to reason about the alignment between claim and evidence at the document level. CLAIM-BENCH targets the *extraction and validation* stage of scientific fact-checking, where the challenge is to locate and structure evidence across a long scientific document. Our work targets a different stage: given the evidence is already isolated, we ask whether the evidence is *sufficient for the strength of the claim*, decomposing sufficiency into a four-class relation taxonomy. Where CLAIM-BENCH asks "can the model find and validate the evidence?", we ask "can the model calibrate the claim's strength to the evidence that is already found?". The two are sequential: CLAIM-BENCH-style extraction feeds our relation-typing calibration, and a complete pipeline would chain the two.

## 4. CESE-OCN differentiation paragraph

CESE-OCN differs from RIGOURATE, ForceBench, and CLAIM-BENCH in three specific ways. First, the **output representation** is a four-class relation label (supported / mild_scope_overclaim / strong_action_overclaim / contradiction_candidate) rather than a continuous scalar (RIGOURATE), a contrastive preference (ForceBench), or a document-level extraction/validation (CLAIM-BENCH). The four-class decomposition isolates *strong_action_overclaim* — claims that assert deployment, safety, policy, or operational conclusions beyond what the evidence warrants — as a separate high-risk boundary class. Second, the **screening orientation** is explicit: R4, our mixed relation-routing framework, is designed as a screening-oriented complement to generic LLM/NLI judges, trading overall macro-F1 for targeted strong_action positive-F1 (R4 strong-F1 = 0.3967 vs. LLM strong-F1 = 0.0769 on matched samples), at the cost of higher review burden. Third, the **data design** is controlled counterfactual: SimClaim pairs real evidence spans from scientific papers with generated claim variants that instantiate specific evidence-sufficiency relations, enabling controlled diagnosis of relation-type separability — in contrast to RIGOURATE's natural author-written claims, ForceBench's paired-claim contrastive design, and CLAIM-BENCH's full-paper extraction setup. Our experimental results (Section §X) show that a scalar overstatement score, whether cue-based or LLM-derived, cannot reliably distinguish mild_scope_overclaim from strong_action_overclaim (ROC-AUC <= 0.59) and cannot match R4's strong_action screening F1, providing empirical support for the four-class decomposition.

## 6. Data section: SimClaim as controlled counterfactual diagnostic set

SimClaim is a controlled counterfactual diagnostic benchmark of 444 claim-evidence pairs across 111 evidence groups and six domains (autonomous driving, NLP, computer vision, reinforcement learning, robotics, and general ML). Unlike natural-claim benchmarks that collect author-written claims from paper abstracts and introductions, SimClaim fixes the evidence span to a real passage from a scientific paper and generates four counterfactual claim variants per evidence group, each instantiating a specific evidence-sufficiency relation: (i) a *supported* claim that the evidence justifies as stated; (ii) a *mild_scope_overclaim* claim that mildly over-extends the evidence's scope without asserting an action conclusion; (iii) a *strong_action_overclaim* claim that asserts a deployment, safety, policy, or operational conclusion the evidence does not warrant; and (iv) a *contradiction_candidate* claim that the evidence directly contradicts. This controlled counterfactual design enables two diagnostic capabilities that natural-claim benchmarks cannot provide: (a) per-relation separability analysis, since the four variants share the same evidence and differ only in claim strength; and (b) high-risk class oversampling, since the 25% strong_action prevalence in SimClaim far exceeds the natural prevalence in real papers, enabling meaningful evaluation of strong_action screening where natural-claim benchmarks would have too few positive cases. The trade-off is realism: SimClaim claims are generated variants, not author-written claims, and a pre-registered realism audit (Section §X) is therefore part of the gold protocol. All silver labels are AI-preannotated and author-screened; gold adjudication is pre-registered and pending.

## 7. Discussion: why scalar overstatement score may be insufficient for high-risk action-overclaim screening

A natural question is whether a continuous scalar overstatement score — as in RIGOURATE — could subsume the four-class relation taxonomy and provide equivalent screening signal for high-risk action overclaims. Our experimental results suggest it cannot, for two reasons. First, a scalar score compresses the *action-vs-scope* distinction: mild_scope_overclaim and strong_action_overclaim differ in the *type* of over-claim (scope expansion vs. action/deployment/safety assertion), not just in the *degree* of overstatement. In our evaluation (Section §X), neither a cue-based scalar (ROC-AUC = 0.5062) nor an LLM-derived scalar (ROC-AUC = 0.5874) could reliably distinguish mild from strong_action, while R4's dedicated strong-action escalation route achieved strong-F1 = 0.3967 on the same samples. Second, scalar scores are threshold-unstable for screening: the best-F1 threshold for strong_action identification differs from the best-F1 threshold for overclaim identification, meaning no single scalar threshold can simultaneously optimise both tasks. R4's structured routing — a contradiction detector followed by a scope-calibration residual classifier and a contradiction-gated strong-action escalation route — provides a type-specific screening signal that scalar compression loses. We therefore position scalar overstatement scoring and relation-specific screening as complementary: scalar for broad severity ranking, relation-specific for high-risk action-overclaim screening. This complementarity is the empirical motivation for CESE-OCN's four-class decomposition.
